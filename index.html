Conceptual Response

The prompt I have chosen is Skeuomorphism, and the technique I have chosen to utilise is date/time : Date.now(). In this project, I will attempt to build a skeuomorphic interface of a vinyl record player that will play different variations of a song depending of the time of day the user is in. The design of the vinyl record will also be be changed every time the song variation changes.

The track I am using is June Time by Patrick Patrikios, which is free to use with the YouTube Audio Library licence. I will be editing the different variations of the song with Reaper. There will be 3 variations in total: original, slowed-down and sped-up.

From an aesthetic perspective, the design of the project is greatly inspired by nu-brutalism, so while the interface will closely replicate the look and functionality of a real record player, it will be designed in a minimalist flat style. I will be using Adobe Illustrator to illustrate the vector elements before importing them into Visual Studio Code.

Technical Approach

For this particular project, I was not inspired by any other existing interfaces, but rather the idea stemmed from my deep interest in album and record design, which eventually lead me to think of creating a digital vinyl record player that could blend modernism and nostalgia, and emulate the peacefulness and warmth of a physical record player. I did a search online of vinyl players, and simplified them into 2D vector graphics in Adobe Illustrator.

After the week 8 lesson, I felt like I should help users learn how to operate my interface, even if my intention was to encourage them to explore and experience the interface with as little instruction as possible. I decided to add an information button that would not stand out too much, but that all users who are exploring the interface would inevitably interact with.

Prototype Testing

During the peer user testing, I was still solving the bugs for the following functions:

  • Start/Stop Button will toggle rotation of the vinyl
  • Volume Slider will control the volume of the main audio
  • Ambient sound button should reflect active state

I also had yet to work on the code for the date() functions, but I got the front-end of the interface looking more or less like what I have planned for the final output for 1 variation. As such, the questions I have prepared for the testing was for me to find out if users are able to grasp the intention of the project, which is to explore, experiment and enjoy a laidback and peaceful audio listening session from the design and foundational features of the prototype.


The two questions were:

  • Did the page make you curious what the page and audio would be like during different parts of the day?
  • Did the page encourage you to interact with the controllers? How intuitive was it for you to use?

[ appearance of prototype during user testing ]

Feedback and Response

[ question 1 ] Did the page make you curious what the page and audio would be like during different parts of the day?

Most testers have indicated that they are curious about how the interface would look like during different times of the day, and I am very happy that my interface has suceeded in this key aspect.

A few testers have suggested that I should display the time adaptation feature more prominantly, such as indicating it on the opening modal or displaying the time and date somewhere on the interface. I think these suggestions are great, and will try to implement a current time display into the interface.

Another piece of feedback I receieved was that I should consider putting the music on loop, as they felt disengaged when the song stopped before they had left the interface. I agree that this is an oversight on my end, and will add loop into the audio controls.

One tester has also suggested that the colour variations complement the mood and vibe of its corresponding time of day, which I will also take into consideration!



[ question 2 ] Did the page encourage you to interact with the controllers? How intuitive was it for you to use?

Understandably, since I could not get a couple of the controls ready for the testing because of the errors, many testers indicated the lack of controls they could interact with on the interface. On the positive side though, most testers said that the interface was intuitive and made them curious about the controllers available.

One tester who found the interface not intuitive suggested that the interactive areas be highlighted in a different colour to make it more obvious to the user what they should be interacting with. While I understand and appreciate the perspective of this tester, I thought that the ambiguity of which controls are fixed and which are interactive plays into the air of mystery I wanted to create for this interface, so that testers would feel curious to explore the controls on their own. However, I do agree that I could have more visual feedback to increase engagement. I could possibly do this by creating hover and active effects for the elements that the users interact with.

Some users also pointed out that they were unsure what the ambient sound button function was. This was understandable as I could not get the active state of the element working in time, and the button was not labelled wih iots function either. While the ambient sound could be successfully toggled through the button, I realise that becasue the volume of the main audio track was too loud, the testers were unable tto observe the change in audio when they interacted with the button. To ameliorate this, I will adjust the volume of the main audio track down when the ambient sound is activated, so that the feedback from the function would be more obvious.

Reflection

I will be implementing the responses to the feedback mentioned above in addition to creating the date.now() features I have planned in my system map. I will also complete the other design and css styling variations required, as well as source the rest of the ambient sounds I wantt to include. It was really great to get fresh and unbiased perspectives on my project through the user-testing exercise, and I truly value the effort that has been made by my peers to provide me with feedback that is constructive and actionable. While I was conscious of the shortcomings of the prototype going into the user-testing, I came out of the experience feeling more confident about completing the project, as I can see that people are able to undersand my concept and intention behind the project, and are having fun engaging with it.
